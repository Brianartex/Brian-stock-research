The Geopolitical Inflection Point — May 2026

The U.S.-Iran war has entered its seventh week. The Strait of Hormuz — through which 20–25% of global oil, 60–70% of Asian naphtha, and significant helium/petrochemical flows pass — is effectively closed. This is not a temporary shock. The IMF, World Food Program, and multiple commodity analysts are now projecting a 18–36 month structural supply contraction across plastics, fertilizers, agricultural inputs, and industrial gases. This report identifies the sectors and stocks best positioned to benefit — directly or indirectly — from these shifts, and presents the full bull and bear case through a council of expert opinions.

SMRs (Small Modular Reactors) are the most exciting structural story in energy.

Why Now: Oil shock + AI power hunger + climate policy tailwind
Iran War Impact: Accelerates nuclear as baseload backup for AI data centers

03 | Semiconductor Materials & Helium — The Invisible Crisis

Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City — producing 27–30% of global helium — has been largely offline since early March following Iranian strikes. The Strait of Hormuz closure means even the helium that can be produced cannot be exported. Helium is irreplaceable in semiconductor manufacturing: wafer cooling during lithography, EUV processes, and chip-on-wafer packaging all depend on helium. South Korea (64.7% Qatar-dependent) is most exposed; TSMC has 80–90% recycling but is not immune to prolonged outage.

04 | Water Infrastructure — Scarcity Drives Investment

Desalination — long considered too expensive — is becoming economically viable as freshwater scarcity worsens globally.

Bottom Line — My Opinion

The Iran war creates a multi-year structural supply shock in fertilizer, industrial gases, and petrochemicals — not a short-term commodity spike. The most durable beneficiaries are companies with pricing power that exists independently of the conflict continuing: nuclear power's AI-driven electricity demand (CCJ, GE Vernova, VST), the secular cybersecurity threat environment (CRWD), and the long-term water infrastructure investment theme (XYL, PNR). The fertilizer and petrochemical plays are excellent near-term trades but are structurally cyclical — buy them when the Iran war premium is being debated, not when urea is at $650 and the news is all bullish. The defense names are already fairly valued and require a ceasefire scare to create a new entry opportunity.
