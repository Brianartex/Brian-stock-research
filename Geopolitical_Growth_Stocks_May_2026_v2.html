The Core Thesis

AI data centers are the largest new source of electricity demand in a generation — and they need power that is continuous, reliable, and zero-carbon to meet corporate sustainability commitments. Solar and wind cannot deliver baseload power at the density AI campuses require. Nuclear is the only proven, dispatchable zero-carbon source that can power a 100MW+ AI campus 24/7. Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta have collectively signed 8–10 nuclear power purchase agreements since 2025. The Iran war accelerates this thesis: oil and gas price spikes make the fixed, predictable cost of nuclear power more economically attractive relative to gas peaker plants. Higher fossil fuel prices widen the cost-of-electricity gap between nuclear and gas, making existing nuclear plants more valuable and new SMR projects more economically viable.

🔬 Deep Dive: Why SMRs Are Different This Time

  • First NRC-Approved Design: NuScale Power is the only SMR developer with an NRC-approved design. Its Carbon Free Power Project (Idaho) was cancelled in 2023 after costs tripled — but lessons are being applied to subsequent projects.
  • Factory Manufacturing: Traditional reactors are bespoke construction projects. SMRs are designed for factory fabrication and modular assembly on-site — the same way Boeing builds airplanes. This should reduce cost overruns and construction timelines.
  • Data Center Proximity: SMRs can be sited closer to load centers (AI campuses) than traditional nuclear sites. Oklo's Aurora design is specifically marketed for industrial micro-siting.
  • Advanced Reactor Designs: Several Generation IV reactor designs (high-temperature gas reactors, molten salt reactors) offer inherent safety features that simplify licensing: no meltdown risk, passively safe shutdown.
  • Ukraine War Precedent: Germany shut its last nuclear plants in 2023 and immediately faced a coal-fired electricity crisis. Europe is now reconsidering nuclear — France is extending reactor life and Poland is building its first nuclear plant.

💰 Deep Dive: The Uranium Supply-Demand Balance

  • Kazatomprom (Kazakhstan): Produces ~40% of Western uranium. 2022 guidance cuts reduced supply by 15%. Even with $100+/lb uranium, bringing new mines online takes 7–10 years.
  • Uranium Spot vs. Contract: Spot price at $106/lb vs. long-term contract prices at ~$80/lb — the gap signals a tighter physical market than contracts reflect. Miners like Cameco are increasingly selling spot at premium prices.
  • Strategic Reserves: No major Western government holds meaningful uranium reserves (unlike oil SPR). There is no strategic uranium stockpile equivalent to the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve.
  • Russia's Role: Russia is a major converter and enricher (not miner). Western utilities relying on Russian conversion/enrichment face supply chain risk — another tailwind for non-Russian supply chain diversification.

The Core Thesis

The Strait of Hormuz closure has cut off approximately 30% of global seaborne urea trade at the precise moment Northern Hemisphere farmers are entering their primary planting season. Urea — the world's most-used nitrogen fertilizer — is now $650/ton, up $80/ton in weeks. The UN World Food Program estimates that if oil prices remain above $100/bbl through mid-2026, the number of people experiencing acute food insecurity globally rises by 80–120 million. North American farmers who locked in fertilizer contracts pre-war have a significant cost advantage over European and Asian farmers who are paying spot prices.

🌾 Deep Dive: The Three Nutrients That Matter

  • Nitrogen (N) — Urea: Most-used fertilizer globally. Produced from natural gas via Haber-Bosch. Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, Oman, Qatar, UAE) are major exporters via Hormuz. Disruption: Direct — Hormuz closure cuts 30% of global urea trade.
  • Phosphate (P): Mined primarily in Morocco/Western Sahara (80% of reserves), US, and Jordan. Disruption: Indirect — energy inflation raises production costs.
  • Potash (K): Dominated by Canpotex (Canada), Belaruskali (Belarus), and Uralkali (Russia). Belarus + Russia together supply 40% of global potash. Disruption: Prior sanctions + Russia-Ukraine war created a structural potash deficit that Iran war now compounds.

🇧🇷 Deep Dive: Brazil — The Swing Factor

  • Brazil's fertilizer import dependency: nitrogen (83%), phosphate (50%), potash (95%) — nearly all imported
  • Brazilian fertilizer suppliers: Russia (28%), Canada (18%), Morocco (15%), USA (10%)
  • The Iran war disrupts Gulf nitrogen supply that feeds Brazilian agriculture via global commodity flows
  • Brazilian farmers signaling they will reduce corn and rice acreage in 2026–2027 due to fertilizer costs
  • Soybean planting in September is Brazil's next major decision point — if soybeans offer better margin per acre vs. corn (due to fertilizer cost), soybean acres shift — affecting global food prices through 2027
  • Key insight: Chinese soybean imports from Brazil will shift to US-sourced supply if Brazil cuts acreage — US farmers benefit, sustaining US fertilizer demand even if global acreage drops

⚠️ Deep Dive: China's Strategic Reserve — The Wild Card

  • China produced ~55M tonnes of urea in 2025 and holds significant strategic reserves
  • Beijing could release reserves to cap global prices — most significant bear case for the fertilizer thesis
  • China imposed export quotas on fertilizer in 2021–2022 during its own food inflation crisis — precedent exists
  • However: Chinese domestic urea prices would fall if exports are unrestricted, reducing Chinese farmer costs — Beijing may prefer to keep domestic prices low
  • The bear case is real but has not materialized in prior Gulf disruption episodes (2019, 2022)

The Core Thesis

Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City — producing 27–30% of global helium — has been largely offline since early March following Iranian strikes. The Strait of Hormuz closure means even the helium that can be produced cannot be exported. Helium is irreplaceable in semiconductor manufacturing: wafer backside cooling during lithography and etching, EUV optical systems, and advanced chip-on-wafer packaging (CoWoS) all depend on helium at specific purities that cannot be substituted.

🔬 Deep Dive: Why Helium Cannot Be Substituted

  • Thermal conductivity: Helium has the highest thermal conductivity of any gas — essential for removing heat from silicon wafers during plasma etching and deposition at nanometer precision.
  • Atomic radius: Helium's small atomic radius makes it the only gas that can penetrate the micro-holes in EUV optical path capillaries without contaminating the optical chain — a unique physical property with no substitute.
  • Chemical inertness: Helium is completely chemically inert at semiconductor process temperatures — the only way to create ultra-pure, non-reactive environments for 3D NAND stacking and HBM manufacturing.
  • Cryogenic cooling: MRI machines, particle accelerators, and certain semiconductor inspection tools require liquid helium for cooling. The medical imaging sector competes with semiconductors for the same helium supply.

🇰🇷 Deep Dive: Regional Exposure Map

  • South Korea (Most Exposed): 64.7% of helium imports from Qatar/Gulf. Samsung and SK Hynix together produce ~70% of global DRAM and HBM memory. Samsung has deployed a Helium Reuse System (HeRS) — the most advanced in the industry — but even this cannot fully offset a 30% supply reduction.
  • Taiwan / TSMC (Moderately Exposed): ~69% of helium imports from Gulf. TSMC's leading-edge fabs (3nm, 2nm) use 80–90% helium recycling rates. CoWoS advanced packaging — critical for AI GPU integration — is the most helium-sensitive step.
  • USA (Least Exposed): ~81 million cubic meters annual domestic production (world's largest). Primary imports from Algeria, not the Gulf. Rudyard, Montana began production early 2026 — the first new US helium source in years.

💰 Deep Dive: Financial Flow — Who Wins, Who Loses

  • Winners: Industrial gas companies (Linde, Air Products, Air Liquide) with existing helium production and long-term contracts. They can sell constrained helium at significantly higher prices.
  • Winners (indirect): Helium recycling equipment makers — Samsung's HeRS and similar systems become critical infrastructure. Lam Research and Applied Materials benefit from equipment orders enabling recycling at fabs.
  • Losers: Memory chip makers (Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron) face rising input costs while trying to price HBM at premium levels to AI customers.
  • Losers (indirect): AI data center builders — HBM shortages delay GPU and AI accelerator production, slowing data center buildout timelines.
  • Wild card: ExxonMobil has large domestic helium capacity at Shute Creek, Wyoming. A wildcard beneficiary if domestic US helium demand surges and pricing follows global spot prices.

The Core Thesis

Water is the ultimate inelastic resource — humans need it to survive, agriculture needs it to grow food, and industry needs it for manufacturing. The UN estimates that by 2030, global water demand will exceed supply by 40% even without geopolitical disruptions. The Iran war accelerates this scarcity dynamic: (1) food production disruptions drive more irrigation/water use, (2) petrochemical-derived water treatment chemicals become more expensive, and (3) geopolitical instability pushes Gulf states to accelerate desalination investment as a national security priority.

In the US, the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law ($1.2 trillion) is finally flowing money to municipal water systems — $55 billion specifically for water infrastructure through 2026. The EPA estimates there is $434 billion in deferred water infrastructure maintenance in the US alone.

🏭 Deep Dive: Desalination — From Expensive to Essential

  • Historical constraint: Desalination has been considered too energy-intensive and expensive — reverse osmosis requires 3–10 kWh per cubic meter of freshwater, compared to ~0.5 kWh for surface water treatment.
  • What changed: Energy costs (from solar/wind) are declining. Simultaneously, freshwater scarcity is making the economics of desalination more favorable: the value of a new freshwater source near a water-stressed city now exceeds the cost of building the plant.
  • Gulf states: Saudi Arabia (NEOM/The Line), UAE, Oman, and Israel are all investing billions in desalination. Saudi Arabia alone produces ~40% of its freshwater from desalination at the Jubail plant (world's largest).
  • US projects: California's Sites Reservoir and the Colorado River shortage are driving new desalination investment in California and Texas.
  • Technology: Forward osmosis, membrane technology (Xylem's Evoqua acquisition), and AI-optimized water treatment systems are reducing costs and energy consumption. IoT sensors (Xylem's smart water network) reduce chemical usage by 15–30%.

🌾 Deep Dive: The Food-Water Nexus

  • Higher fertilizer prices → farmers may switch to irrigation-intensive crops (rice, cotton) to maximize revenue per acre → increased water demand
  • Petrochemical shortages (plastics) affect drip irrigation systems — PVC pipe and plastic liner production is constrained by the same naphtha shortage hitting fertilizers
  • Food price inflation (from lower yields) increases the political urgency of water infrastructure investment — governments prioritize food security
  • Nitrogen fertilizer production (Haber-Bosch) is extremely water-intensive — ~5 tonnes of water per tonne of ammonia produced. Higher nitrogen production = more water stress.

The Core Thesis

The Strait of Hormuz closure has disrupted 60–70% of Asian naphtha trade — naphtha being the primary feedstock for petrochemical production across Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and Southeast Asia. Japan has already curtailed petrochemical output. Without naphtha and its derivative ethylene/propylene, the production of polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), polyvinyl chloride (PVC), and dozens of other polymers becomes more expensive or impossible.

US ethane-based ethylene producers — using cheap Appalachian and Texas natural gas liquids as feedstock — have a massive cost advantage. Ethane is priced at ~30–40% of naphtha on an energy-equivalent basis. This creates a structural arbitrage: US ethane-based polymers can be produced at $400–500/ton lower cost than Asian naphtha-based production.

🧪 Deep Dive: The Chemical Feedstock Stack

  • Naphtha (oil derivative, Gulf-dependent): Primary feedstock for ~60% of global ethylene. $700–900/ton in Asia currently.
  • Ethane (natural gas, US-abundant): Primary US feedstock. $200–300/ton. ~40% cheaper than naphtha on an energy-equivalent basis.
  • Methanol (natural gas, US/Middle East): Another Gulf-dependent feedstock. US Gulf methanol at $300–400/ton vs. Asian methanol at $500–600/ton.
  • Bamboo/Corn-based bioplastics (biomass, China-scaling): PLA (polylactic acid from corn starch) and bamboo fiber composites are the most commercially mature alternatives. Not yet cost-competitive with oil-based plastics but the cost gap is closing as oil prices rise and scale improves.

🌿 Deep Dive: Bio-Based Plastics — The China Scale-Up Story

  • Bamboo fiber composites: China has 1/3 of the world's bamboo reserves. Bamboo grows 30x faster than trees, requires no fertilizer (beyond trace nitrogen), and produces a fiber that can be blended with PLA to create strong, cheap, biodegradable plastic substitutes. Total advanced biolab production capacity in China: 2M+ tonnes/year under construction as of 2026.
  • Corn-based PLA: NatureWorks (Cargill/Dairy Farmers of America) and TotalEnergies Corbion are the largest PLA producers. Chinese companies (Henan Jonce, Ecomann) scaling fast. PLA is already cost-competitive with polypropylene in certain applications (packaging, 3D printing filament).
  • Agricultural waste: Rice straw, wheat straw, and sugarcane bagasse into biopolymers. Not yet at scale but the technology is maturing.
  • Market size: Bioplastics currently represent ~1% of global plastic production (35M tonnes of 400M total). By 2030, estimates range from 3% to 8% depending on oil price trajectory.
  • Regulatory tailwind: EU Single-Use Plastics Directive, China's "Double Carbon" policy (carbon peak 2030, carbon neutral 2060), and US state-level plastic bans create regulatory demand for biodegradable alternatives.

🏭 Deep Dive: US Gulf Coast Export Arbitrage

  • Export infrastructure: The key constraint for US ethane-based producers is export terminal capacity. Cheniere Energy (LNG), Enterprise Products, and others have been building ethane export terminals. ~1.5M barrels/day of ethane export capacity under construction or planned through 2028.
  • The irony: The Iran war causes oil price spikes that (a) make US ethane cheaper relative to naphtha, AND (b) accelerate the adoption of bioplastics as a cost-effective alternative. US chemical companies and bioplastics companies both benefit from the same disruption.

The Core Thesis

The Iran war represents the largest sustained US military engagement since the post-9/11 era. Unlike the Iraq/Afghanistan era, where much of the spending was personnel-intensive ground operations, the Iran conflict is capital-intensive: missiles, precision-guided munitions, air defense systems, naval assets, and cyber warfare tools. This is exactly the kind of conflict where defense contractors generate exceptional revenue per dollar of government spending.

🚀 Deep Dive: The Three Conflict Categories

  • Kinetic/Conventional: Tomahawk land-attack missiles ($1–2M each), JDAM-guided bombs ($25–30K), AIM-120 air-to-air missiles. The US has fired hundreds of Tomahawks. Restocking the National Defense Stockpile requires new production contracts — RTX (Tomahawk manufacturer), LMT (JDAM, AIM-120), GD (naval munitions) all benefit.
  • Air Defense: Iron Dome, David's Sling, and THAAD interceptors ($50K–100K per intercept) are being expended at high rates to defend against Iranian drones and missiles. The Iron Dome production line (Rafael Advanced Defense Systems, co-produced with Raytheon RTX) is running at maximum capacity.
  • Cyber: Iranian state-sponsored hackers (MuddyWater, Phosphorus, Emennet Pasandar) are actively targeting US financial systems, power grid/SCADA systems, defense contractors, and healthcare. Nation-state cyber attacks generate sustained procurement budgets for Zero Trust architecture — CrowdStrike, Palo Alto Networks, Zscaler.

🌍 Deep Dive: Allied Spending Surge

  • F-35 Sales: Lockheed Martin's F-35 program has GCC customers (UAE, Israel, Saudi Arabia) accelerating purchases. The UAE deal (50 F-35s, ~$10B) was in negotiation for years — the Iran war may finalize this.
  • Naval: Gulf states are purchasing littoral combat ships, patrol vessels, and anti-ship missiles to secure their coastlines.
  • Missile Defense: THAAD (RTX) and Patriot (RTX/LMT) batteries are being purchased by Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Japan at accelerated pace. Japan's acquisition of additional THAAD batteries (announced March 2026) is directly linked to the Iran war threat assessment.
  • Cyber: Allied nations in the Gulf are building national cybersecurity centers — US CYBERCOM's "defend forward" doctrine means more contract work for US cyber companies operating in allied territories.

The Core Thesis

China and broader Emerging Markets are the most complex, highest-risk, and potentially highest-reward beneficiary of the Iran war supply shock. The thesis is not a single stock story — it is a macro thesis about which industrial base is best positioned to fill the void left by disrupted Western and Gulf production capacity. China is largely energy-independent (its own coal, nuclear, and increasingly renewables), has the world's largest manufacturing capacity, and is aggressively scaling technologies that bypass oil-based petrochemical supply chains entirely.

The bear case is severe: China faces a prolonged property sector crisis, demographic headwinds, sovereign default risk, geopolitical tensions with the West, and capital controls that limit US investor access. But for patient investors willing to accept ADR risk, currency risk, and regulatory risk, there are opportunities that do not exist in Western markets.

🎋 Deep Dive: Bamboo Bioplastics — China's Industrial Scale-Up

  • China's bamboo reserves: China holds approximately 1/3 of the world's bamboo — 5 million hectares across Sichuan, Zhejiang, Hunan, Jiangxi, and Fujian provinces. Bamboo grows 30x faster than trees (harvest cycle: 3–5 years vs. 20–60 years), requires no synthetic fertilizer (it is naturally nitrogen-fixing), and can grow on degraded land unsuitable for agriculture.
  • How it works: Bamboo cellulose is extracted via mechanical or chemical pulping, then processed through esterification to create cellulose acetate or blended with PLA (polylactic acid from corn starch) to create composite materials. The resulting materials are biodegradable, mechanically competitive with polypropylene in many applications, and cost-competitive at $1,800–2,200/ton vs. $1,400–1,800/ton for oil-based PP.
  • Industrial capacity: As of 2026, China has 2.3M tonnes/year of "advanced bamboo/paper-based bioplastic" production capacity under construction or recently commissioned, primarily in Sichuan and Zhejiang provinces. The Chinese government has designated this as a "strategic emerging industry" under the 14th Five-Year Plan, qualifying it for preferential tax treatment, subsidized loans, and direct investment grants.
  • Market applications: Single-use packaging (straws, cutlery, food containers), agricultural mulch film, 3D printing filament, automotive interior non-structural components, consumer electronics housings.
  • Who benefits: There is no pure-play US-traded bamboo bioplastic stock. However, companies with China manufacturing exposure or joint ventures in this space could benefit. Investors can access via KraneShares CSI China Internet ETF (KWEB) or specific China materials funds.

⚡ Deep Dive: China's EV/Battery Supply Chain Dominance

  • Catalyst: The Iran war causes oil price spikes, which accelerate EV adoption economics in price-sensitive markets (Southeast Asia, India, Eastern Europe, China itself). Higher gasoline prices narrow the cost gap between EVs and ICE vehicles faster than expected.
  • CATL (Contemporary Amperex Technology): Produces ~37% of global EV battery capacity. CATL's CTP (cell-to-pack) and LFP (lithium iron phosphate) technology is lower-cost than competitors.
  • Lithium supply: Chile (SQM, Albemarle) and Australia (Pilbara Minerals) dominate lithium supply. China controls significant lithium processing capacity through Tianqi Lithium and Ganfeng Lithium — the conversion from raw lithium carbonate/hydroxide to battery-grade material is concentrated in China.
  • The geopolitical risk: The US IRA (Inflation Reduction Act) and proposed CHIPS-like decoupling legislation could restrict Chinese battery technology from US EV supply chains. This is the most significant bear case: Western subsidy programs explicitly exclude Chinese content, creating a bifurcated global supply chain.
  • The opportunity: CATL, BYD, and other Chinese battery makers dominate the Southeast Asian, Indian, European, and Chinese domestic EV markets — representing 70%+ of the global EV market. US exclusion of Chinese content affects the remaining 30%.

🌏 Deep Dive: Emerging Markets Beyond China

  • Brazil (Agriculture): Brazilian farmers face fertilizer cost headwinds but have access to US/Canadian potash. Brazilian agricultural exporters (JBS, BRF, Vale fertilizer offtake) could capture Chinese import demand if US-Brazil agricultural trade flows shift.
  • India (Pharmaceuticals + IT): India is the world's largest generic drug manufacturer — API (active pharmaceutical ingredient) supply from India is becoming more critical as Gulf chemical supply chains tighten. India's IT services sector (Infosys, TCS) could capture more US defense/cybersecurity contracting as US firms diversify away from China.
  • Indonesia (Nickel + Energy): Indonesia controls ~25% of global nickel reserves — the key input for lithium-ion batteries. Indonesia is positioning itself as a battery manufacturing hub through the EV battery corridor in Sulawesi and Kalimantan. Indonesia's HPAL (high-pressure acid leach) nickel processing technology is the lowest-cost in the world.
  • Saudi Arabia (Petrochemicals): Saudi Aramco and SABIC are investing heavily in non-oil chemical production to diversify away from oil export revenue. The Iran war strengthens this strategic motivation — SABIC's US Gulf Coast assets are now advantaged vs. Asian naphtha-based producers.

⚠️ Deep Dive: The Bear Cases Are Severe — Know Them Well

  • China ADR delisting risk: The Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act (HFCAA) requires PCAOB access to audit workpapers. If Chinese companies refuse PCAOB access, they face trading prohibitions. VIE structures used by Chinese ADRs are legally ambiguous and could be invalidated.
  • Capital controls: Renminbi is not freely convertible. US investors in Chinese ADRs face currency risk and limited ability to repatriate capital in a crisis.
  • Property sector contagion: China's property sector crisis (Evergrande, Country Garden, Sunac defaults) has not fully resolved. Property represents ~25–30% of China's GDP when including related sectors.
  • Tariff/decoupling risk: The IRA explicitly excludes Chinese EV battery content from US EV tax credits. Further CHIPS-like legislation could restrict Chinese semiconductor, AI, and battery technology from US and allied markets.
  • Geopolitical flashpoints: Taiwan Strait tensions are the ultimate black swan for Chinese equity exposure. A Taiwan crisis would trigger an immediate, severe selloff in all Chinese equities with no guarantee of recovery.

The One-Paragraph Summary

The Iran war has created a structural supply shock across seven interconnected sectors. Nuclear energy benefits from a perfect collision of AI power demand and fossil fuel price inflation. Fertilizer producers capture near-term windfall margins from the planting-season supply disruption. Semiconductor materials face a real but manageable helium crunch. Water infrastructure is a 5–10 year secular investment. Petrochemical alternatives (US ethane-based producers and bioplastics) benefit from a feedstock arbitrage that could persist for 18–36 months. Defense contractors are direct conflict beneficiaries. And China — the most contrarian call — is positioned to capture disrupted markets through industrial scale, energy independence, and aggressive scaling of oil-free plastic alternatives. The bamboo bioplastics story alone, while tiny today, is the most interesting long-term structural narrative in global materials. The risk is that the market knows all of this, and valuations have already compressed the opportunity significantly.
