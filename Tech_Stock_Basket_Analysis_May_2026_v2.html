This expanded v2 report provides deep-dive research on 16 companies across three tiers — 9 Tier 1 companies (from v1) plus 7 additional companies in Tier 2 and Tier 3 that merit consideration based on similar financial and price characteristics. The core thesis holds that a category of high-quality software companies trading at historically distressed valuations represents one of the most compelling risk/reward setups in public equity markets in years.

Core Thesis — v2 Expanded

Buy a basket of 20–30 equal-weight names fitting these criteria — profitable or approaching profitability, growing, generating free cash flow, with fortress balance sheets — trading 70%+ below their 2021 peaks. Hold for 5+ years. The market is wrong about AI "killing" these businesses, and the cash flow statements will eventually prove it. The key variable this report adds to pure financial analysis: price momentum and reversal confirmation. A stock can be cheap and fundamentally solid — but if it's still in a downtrend with no signs of reversal, it can stay cheap for years.

Tier Definitions:
• Tier 1 — The original 9 names, fitting the strict criteria most precisely (70%+ below 2021 highs, profitable or approaching profitability, strong FCF).
• Tier 2 — Companies that either narrowly miss one criterion (VEEV is only ~47% below ATH) or are early in their turnaround arc, but where the financial quality is exceptional and the price is compelling.
• Tier 3 — Higher-risk/higher-scrutiny names that may be relevant for a broader basket but require more caution: either GAAP-unprofitable, or facing structural competitive challenges, or with an acquisition/PE event altering the investment thesis.

📊 Sector Analysis: Is the "AI Will Kill SaaS" Thesis Real?

Before analyzing individual stocks, we must address the central premise: Are these companies facing cyclical disdain (which creates the opportunity) or secular decline (which would make them value traps)? We examine both sides rigorously.

Bear Case for "AI Will Kill SaaS" — Is Overblown

  • Enterprise software has structural moats: Deep integrations, customer data, workflow embedded in processes — can't be swapped out by AI prompts alone
  • AI is an enhancement, not a replacement: Goldman Sachs March 2026 "Will AI Eat Software?" analysis concluded AI is more likely to reinforce existing software incumbents than displace them
  • Switching costs are real: These companies have millions of users, petabytes of customer data, and enterprise contracts that don't get cancelled because a new AI tool launched
  • Actual renewal rates hold: Customer concentration and renewal rate data contradicts the "everyone is leaving" narrative
  • AWS CEO Matt Garman (Feb 2026): Publicly stated AI disruption fears for SaaS are overblown

Bull Case for "AI Will Kill SaaS" — Risks Are Real

  • Microsoft bundles Power Automate + Copilot free with Office 365/Azure — displaces UiPath, Atlassian, and others from the enterprise stack
  • Freemium AI agents can replicate workflows these platforms charge for — unit economics of incumbents are threatened
  • Data gravity is eroding: Snowflake's moat vs. Microsoft Fabric — Fabric is deeply integrated into Office 365/Azure, making data gravity less relevant
  • Video conferencing was "commoditized" — Zoom went from 50x P/E to a value stock in 3 years — the same can happen to other categories
  • The "forced selling" mechanism is weaker now: Many hedge funds already rotated out in 2022–2025 — who is left to do the forced selling that creates the ideal setup?
Goldman Sachs — "Will AI Eat Software?" (March 2026)

Goldman's 31-page analysis concluded that AI is more likely to become a reinforcement for most enterprise software than a replacement. For horizontal productivity software (Jira, Confluence, Zoom), the risks are higher. For vertical software with deep workflow integration and switching costs (DocuSign, Okta, Veeva), the moats hold. The report specifically noted that bundling by hyperscalers (Microsoft Fabric, Google Workspace AI) represents the most credible existential threat to several names in this basket — and that the threat is most acute for companies without strong vertical differentiation.

🔍 Counter-Analysis: Flaws, Weak Spots, and Things That Could Go Wrong

⚠️ Critical Flaw #1: The "Forced Selling" Mechanism May Be Weak in 2026 vs. 2002

The 2002 analog works because hedge funds that owned tech in 1999 were FORCED to sell in 2001–2002 as their LBO funds expired and public market tech exposure triggered fund-level margin calls. In 2026, most institutional tech investors have already rotated out of these names over the past 3–4 years. The forced selling from 2022–2025 may have already accomplished the equivalent of what happened in 2002. If true, there is no "forced seller" left to create the final washout that makes the basket entry so powerful.

⚠️ Critical Flaw #2: "AI Will Kill SaaS" May Actually Be True This Time

The 2002 analogy breaks down on a crucial point: in 2002, the internet companies that got crushed were impaired but ultimately survived because the internet itself wasn't going to eat their lunch. In 2026, the AI threat is different in character: if a natural language AI agent can complete the workflow that Jira, Asana, Zoom, DocuSign, and UiPath charge for, the "switching cost moat" is not a permanent barrier — it's just a temporary friction that AI is actively working to eliminate. The comparison to 2002 may be too optimistic.

⚠️ Critical Flaw #3: Most Names Are Only 65–75% Down, Not 80–90%

The thesis calls for "70% to 85% below 2021 highs." In reality, many names are only at the 65–75% decline range. This means the market has already done meaningful work in repricing these names. The ones that are truly 85%+ down (RingCentral, Twilio, Zoom) have specific structural problems that may justify further decline. The ones with cleaner balance sheets (DocuSign, Okta, Monday.com) are only 73–79% down — the easy money may have already been made.

⚠️ Critical Flaw #4: Dollar-Cost Averaging Is Required, Not Optional

Buying a "basket" implies equal weighting at one point in time. But the historical precedent requires buying continuously throughout a multi-year bottoming period. If DocuSign drops another 30% next quarter, buying equal weights today means putting significant capital into a falling knife. True discipline requires a pre-committed DCA schedule, not a one-time basket purchase.

⚠️ Critical Flaw #5: The Basket Requires 20–30 Names; We Analyzed 16

The original thesis calls for a 20–30 name basket specifically to diversify away company-specific risk. With only 16 names analyzed (and 2–3 of those are M&A/PE situations that should be removed), this report has done roughly 50–65% of the necessary work. The remaining 4–14 names in a real basket may look very different from the ones analyzed here. Some may be better entries; some may be worse.

  • Microsoft Copilot/Entra/Fabric fails to meaningfully displace at least one of the key names

  • Any company in the basket announcing additional layoffs or business restructuring beyond what's already disclosed
  • BILL or PATH continuing to make new 52-week lows through Q2 2026 — would suggest the "bath" has further to go
